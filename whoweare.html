The idea of starting Hoitinga & van Zwieten (HvZ) Computational Engineering started in 2010. At the time we were both PhD candidates at Delft University of Technology in the Engineering Mechanics group of Prof. René de Borst. The primary research topics in that group were related to modeling and numerical methods, associated with solid and fluid mechanical problems in engineering.

Over time we realized that a combined thorough knowledge of mathematical modeling and numerical methods is rare. Meanwhile, the world of virtual prototyping had been steadily gaining traction, for which both the topics are key elements. We then decided to make the bold step, to continue on what we like to do best, and to try and assist that ever increasing interest in virtual prototyping.

Wijnand Hoitinga

In 2001 I obtained my Bachelor's degree in Aerospace Engineering and 2004 a Master's degree in Aerodynamics. During my masters, I got familiarized with numerical methods, in particular the Least-Squares Spectral-Element method (LSQSEM). Under the supervision of Marc Gerritsma I studied possible improvements on the LSQSEM method, which eventually led to the so-called Direct Minimization LSQSEM. A copy of my master thesis can be found here (pdf) and a journal paper is available here.

Just having finished my masters, I began to work as a development engineer at ASML in the flow and temperature control group. ASML is world leader in the field of lithography systems for the semiconductor industry, and serves a high-tech industry with many engineering challenges. As such, ASML has been a valuable experience in which I learned many aspects regarding a pragmatic working attitude and experimental design validation.

To improve my analytical skills, in mid 2006 I decided to start a Ph.D. at the Delft University in the Engineering Mechanics group of Prof. René de Borst under the supervision of Prof. Harald van Brummelen. Currently, Prof. de Borst chairs Numerical Methods in Engineering and Prof. Van Brummelen chairs Multiscale Engineering Fluid Dynamics, both at the Eindhoven University.

From 2006 to 2010 I studied on how to model rarefied gas flows and, more importantly, how to efficiently solve such models with numerical methods, in particular by using a-posteriori error-estimation techniques and goal-adaptive finite-element discretization. Publications of this work can be found here and here (pdf). Modeling rarefied gas flows remains a challenge, especially for heat-transfer problems on very small scales. Nevertheless, my research led to a better understanding in rarefied flow modeling and, moreover, some promising future steps that are worth investigating.

With experience in both industry and academia, I have decided to provide consultancy services on modeling and simulation to industry, yet with an eye on academia in that the latest developments on numerical methods are used. Together with Gertjan van Zwieten, currently working as a postdoc at the Eindhoven University, I decided to help industry with their product design by means of virtual prototyping. To this end, Gertjan and I founded Hoitinga & van Zwieten Computational Engineering.

Gertjan van Zwieten

I started my university education at the faculty of Aerospace Engineering of TU Delft. After my BSc I made a switch to mathematics, again at TU Delft, where I received my MSc degree following an internship at Habanera Numerical Software (recently joined with Dynaflow), for which I was supervised by Prof. Vuik and Dr. Van Gijzen. The work aimed at accelerating the solution of large linear systems originating from fluid mechanics, in particular by the application of deflation preconditioning. More information about this work can be found here.

Currently I am pursuing my PhD degree in the field of numerical methods in engineering, under supervision of Prof. Hanssen and Prof. Gutiérrez. I expect to defend my thesis late 2011. The project involves the numerical modeling of tectonic fault zones, with the aim of obtaining a better understanding of the precise mechanisms behind earthquakes. The main outcome of this work is a novel finite element description that allows for considering several different fault geometries with very little overhead. Publications regarding this work will be added to this page soon.

Meanwhile I worked at Hansje Brinker BV, where I helped establish a new computing framework for satellite monitoring of coastal defence structures. As of May 2011 I am working as a postdoc at TU Eindhoven, where I work on a project for Océ. My current main field of study is the Boundary Element Method for fluid problems. This newly built up expertise, as well as the software development involved, are expected to be highly relevant for our work at HvZ.
